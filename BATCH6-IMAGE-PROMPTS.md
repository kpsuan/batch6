# Q21-Q23 Image Generation Prompts for Gemini

This document contains all options from Q21, Q22, and Q23 (Section 5: End of Life Planning) with their "How This Sounds" descriptions and corresponding Gemini image generation prompts.

## IMPORTANT: Diversity and Photography Requirements

**For ALL prompts below, append the following instructions:**

> "Shot on Kodak Portra 400 or Fujifilm Pro 400H film. Photorealistic, not illustration or cartoon. Real human subjects. Warm film grain, natural skin tones.
>
> **Rotate diversity across images:**
> - Ethnicities: Filipino, Indian, Nigerian, American, British, Chinese, Southeast Asian (Vietnamese, Thai, Indonesian), Latino, Middle Eastern
> - Include same-sex couples (two men or two women) in 20-30% of couple/family scenes
> - Include people with visible disabilities (wheelchair users, prosthetic limbs, mobility aids, hearing aids, visual impairment canes) in 15-20% of images
> - Vary ages, body types, and backgrounds"

## Q21: Dying Location Preferences
**Question:** When your time comes, where would you feel most at peace?

**Question Title Image Prompt:** Create a contemplative photorealistic photograph of a Filipino woman in her 50s, a hospice nurse, standing at the window of her small apartment at dawn, looking out over a city that became home after she left her province decades ago. One hand rests on the glass. Behind her on the wall, a framed photograph of a house she grew up in and a map torn from a magazine. The question of where she would feel most at peace lives in the distance between the glass and that photograph -- two places, two versions of belonging, and a body that can only be in one. Early morning light bisected by curtain shadow, the solitude of someone mapping their final geography. Shot on Kodak Portra 400 film. Style: documentary photography, contemplative, geographic, showing that choosing where to die means choosing which version of home your body will occupy.

#### Option 1: At home, in the place I have built my life
**How This Sounds:** I want to die in this home I built with years I cannot get back -- the walls I painted after double shifts, the kitchen where I cook food that reminds me who I was before I left. I have watched patients die in hospital beds reaching for something familiar and I know what they were reaching for. My family would carry every hour of my dying here, and I know what those hours cost because I have counted them beside other families. That terrifies me. But this is my place.

**Gemini Prompt:** Create a tender photorealistic photograph of a Nigerian woman in her 50s, an ICU nurse, sitting on the floor of her kitchen in the apartment she has lived in for fifteen years, back against the cabinet she installed herself, hands resting on tiles she chose after a double shift. Her expression holds pride and weight equally: this place is hers, and dying here means asking her family to carry hours she has counted beside other families. Evening kitchen light, the warmth of a life built by hand. Shot on Kodak Portra 400 film. Style: documentary photography, tender, rooted, showing that choosing to die at home means choosing the place your hands built and asking the people you love to hold you inside it.

## Q22: Hospice and Palliative Care Philosophy
**Question:** How do you feel about hospice and palliative care?

Style: documentary photography, reflective, reversal, showing that the question of how you feel about hospice lands differently when you stop explaining it for others and start sitting with it for yourself.
